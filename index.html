Santorio: Rebuilding a Growth Engine From Zero

718,370 SAR in subscription revenue from a total monthly marketing budget of roughly 5,000 SAR — 3,000 of which went to Google Ads alone — alongside 240%+ overall sales growth and organic Instagram growth from under 1,000 to 6,000 followers in one year.

Context

I joined Santorio in 2021, originally responsible for social media design, content, and paid media management. At the time, there was no marketing team — only the founders and the kitchen/operations staff. Santorio (a healthy meal subscription brand based in Jeddah and Makkah) had no real presence on social media, no organized subscription or menu management system, and no structured advertising.

The Problem

Three compounding issues: the only sales lever the brand used was constant discounting, which eroded margins; there was no conversion tracking of any kind, so ad spend was effectively blind; and subscription/menu management for each client was manual and outdated, limiting how many customers the business could serve well.

Strategy

I built a fully integrated Salla e-commerce store, allowing customers to select subscription plans and complete payment without manual back-and-forth. I connected the store to every advertising platform for accurate conversion measurement, and layered in Google Tag Manager, Google Analytics, and Microsoft Clarity for heatmap and session analysis to continuously improve the purchase flow.

On acquisition, I built an always-on Google Ads campaign alongside ongoing SEO work — eventually reaching the #1 organic search position in Jeddah and Makkah without paid promotion. I built customer personas from existing data, broke down every subscription package, and assigned each its own targeted audience, content identity, and custom ad creative — rather than running one generic campaign for the whole brand.

Execution & Growth Loop

Each campaign fed into a feedback loop: I gave customer service guidelines for upselling and feedback capture, which fed back into creative and targeting decisions. Pricing was restructured gradually and professionally — moving away from constant discounting toward seasonal promotions only, while protecting loyal customers with long-term subscription discounts. As the brand stabilized, I helped build a dedicated social team, then shifted focus to new growth channels: a partnership with Saudia Airlines' 'Mazaya' program, and a full integration with the Yumealz app, where I personally managed packages, photography, UI design, and coordination with their technical team.

Obstacles

The hardest constraint was budget: proving real growth on a total monthly spend of roughly 5,000 SAR meant no room for inefficient testing. Every persona, segmentation, and creative had to be deliberate from the start — which is why that work happened before any meaningful scaling of spend, not after.

Reflection

This project taught me that infrastructure has to come before scale. Most brands want to scale ad spend before they can even measure what spend is doing — we did the opposite, and a ~5,000 SAR/month budget outperformed what far larger unstructured budgets typically achieve, because every riyal was traceable to a specific persona, package, and creative.

Asmara Agency: From Business Development to De Facto GM

Joined as a business developer to bring in new projects. Left having rebuilt the agency's department structure, founded its design division as a primary revenue line, and been appointed informal administrative lead working directly alongside the CEO.

Context & Problem

Asmara was a Saudi creative agency specializing almost exclusively in motion graphics and animation. I joined to develop new business — but once inside, I identified a structural gap: no organized graphic design department, despite design being a far faster revenue cycle than the slow, resource-intensive animation work a young agency needed for steady cash flow. The wider organization also had no clear departmental structure.

Strategy & Execution

I stepped away from my original business development mandate to art-direct the graphic design department until it ran independently — to the point that design revenue became a primary funding source covering the rest of the agency, including animation. I then took over leadership of the motion department too, maintaining quality control across both.

As project volume grew without clear structure, I built a dedicated HR function from scratch — personally recruiting the HR hire, aligning them with company goals, and working individually with them to fix issues across every department, including project timeline planning, leave policy, and outdated employee contracts that protected neither side adequately.

Results

  • Founded and scaled a graphic design department that became a primary revenue source
  • Took over and stabilized the motion/animation department
  • Built the agency's first HR function and resolved structural issues across every department
  • Rebuilt all employee contracts to protect both company and staff
  • Appointed de facto administrative co-lead by the CEO

Reflection

This was less a marketing project and more proof that I can see and fix organizational problems beyond my job title when the business needs it — and that creative leadership and operational leadership are not separate skill sets for me.

Sha'er Al-Raya: Creative Direction Under National Visibility

Personally recruited and directed the visual production team for Season 4 of one of Saudi Arabia's largest cultural TV programs — with quality improvements specifically praised by the show's management.

عينات من محتوى الفيديو للموسم:

Context & Problem

Ahead of Season 4 of Sha'er Al-Raya, broadcast by the Saudi Broadcasting Authority (SBA), the founder of Creative 360 brought me in to assemble and direct a full production team — designers, motion artists, and video editors — for the show's social media visual identity. Previous seasons had specific, identifiable design weaknesses, especially around visual material availability under tight broadcast timelines.

Strategy & Execution

I studied previous seasons in detail to identify exactly where designs fell short, then built a complete art direction framework and recruited talent specifically matched to executing that vision — while keeping tight control of the team budget to stretch it as far as possible.

To solve the recurring gap in available visual material under broadcast deadlines, I made the strategic call to incorporate AI-assisted creative tools into the workflow — closing production gaps without sacrificing visual quality or consistency, even when source material was limited or last-minute.

Results

  • Program management personally praised the visual quality improvement over previous seasons
  • Successfully delivered the full pre-broadcast visual identity and content system for the season

Note: direct responsibility covered the creative direction phase leading up to broadcast; ongoing in-season and post-broadcast content was managed by a different team after handover.

Reflection

This project is clear proof that I can lead creative direction at a national, brand-sensitive scale — recruiting the right team, managing budget discipline, and solving production constraints creatively rather than letting them lower the quality bar.

About

Design and business were never two different worlds.

I started in design — out of a genuine passion for art and creativity. But I studied Management Information Systems, which gave me a parallel education in business logic, systems thinking, and strategy. Most people see design and business as two different worlds. I never did.

Every brand problem I've solved since — whether it was rebuilding a struggling F&B brand's entire growth engine, restructuring a creative agency's internal operations, or directing the visual identity for a national cultural program — has come from refusing to separate the two. Good marketing is a creative problem. Good design is a business decision.
